Gender Inequalities in American College Sports

Aims: The aim of this project is to explore gender inequalities in the finances of American college sports. Inequality is well documented in sports, and in a new development from 2021, athletes have been allowed to be paid sponsorships and other endorsements during their college careers for the first time. This has created a new market, currently with little legislation, in an area already struggling with disparate outcomes. Understanding the scale of potential issues is an important step to solving them.

Data: The majority of the data for this project comes from the US Department of Education’s Equity in Athletics Database (EIA) and the Knight-Newhouse College Athletics dataset (KNCA), downloaded directly from the sites in csv form. All the data was then cleaned using Colab in Python and hosted on my Github. Other data for NIL valuation was scraped from the On3 database. The scraper is linked here and is constructed in a way that when they have more data points for other sports, the scraper can be easily amended to collect them.

US College sports aside from football, generally lose money. The structure of US sports inherently will result in inequal outcomes as by far the most popular sport football, is almost exclusively played by men. This is evident in the profitability of college sports, aside from football, only men’s basketball makes money.

Conclusions

As college sports generally lose money, colleges' decisions on what sports get funded have scope to be based on what will bring the most wider value to the college. Women’s sports should not be disadvantaged for financial reasons, especially when a lot of the funding comes from student fees and government funding, rather than the sporting activities alone.

Inequality gets worse as the sums of money involved get bigger, so addressing inequality in a top-down way might prove most beneficial. With the advent of a more professional setting for college sports, creating more equal earning opportunities at the top may have a trickle-down effect. A focus on diversity in leadership positions is also a potential area of focus.

As women are underrepresented in the college populus, tackling sporting inequality might require a greater understanding of inequality throughout US colleges, as an area of further research.
